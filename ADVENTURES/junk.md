




The front door gives a cheerful jingle as you step up—something between a bell and a tiny chime-harp. Warmth rolls out, thick with steeped herbs and earthy spice.

A voice calls from inside, bright and unbothered.

Merriwig: “If you’re here to buy tea, come in. If you’re here to steal tea… come in, and I’ll watch you do it.”

--

When you enter, the common room is round and cozy, lantern-lit even in midday. A gnome in a stained apron stands behind a long counter lined with kettles and jars—each labeled in neat script. His hair is a pale puff of frizz, his goggles shoved up like a crown.

Merriwig: “Shoes off if you’d like. Boots on if you must. Either way—sit. You look like the forest tried to season you.”

His eyes flick to the spores on your cloaks—then to Aillig’s watery, bloodshot gaze. He makes an approving little noise, like a chef seeing a proper bruise.

Merriwig: “Ah. You’ve met my children. Breathe through your mouth, dear. Less… friendship.”
He slides a small cup across to Aillig.
Merriwig: “Drink. Bitter. Effective.”

In the corner, a nervous man with a tinkerer’s cart half-unpacked startles at your entrance and then immediately stands, as if he might need to introduce himself to the wall.

Pell: “P-Pell. Pell Whistock. Just Pell. Hello.”
He says it again, softer, like practice. “Pell.”

Merriwig waves him down without looking.

Merriwig: “Pell, breathe. They’re guests, not tax collectors.”

He pours tea with practiced ease—then pauses as a name, or a description, lands.
---

Merriwig (more careful now): “You said… Vaelith.”
He sets the kettle down like it’s suddenly heavier.
Merriwig: “Yes. I knew her. Brilliant. Stubborn. Could’ve argued a stone into blooming.”

He leans on the counter, eyes distant—seeing a different room, a different table, a different sort of rot.

Merriwig: “Months, we worked. Mycelium and spellwork. Mycoremediation—magic-fed, fast-growing, hungry enough to eat the rot itself. We’d run trials in my lab… then she’d race off to the Swamp to test it in the field.”
Merriwig: “It helped. Until it didn’t.”

He taps two fingers on a jar of pale grey powder. The sound is sharp in the cozy room.

Merriwig: “The corruption wasn’t just… dragon stink in the air. It’s in the bones of the land. Ley lines under the forest. It bled into them and spread like a cancer.”
Merriwig: “My craft hit a ceiling. My fungi could only do so much without stronger magic… or rarer stock… or both.”

He exhales, then forces brightness back into his voice like stirring sugar into bitter tea.

Merriwig: “So Vaelith went looking elsewhere. Last I knew, she was headed to Pinewood—talking to folk there. She wouldn’t say who. Only that someone ‘knew the river’s secrets.’”

He looks over each of you in turn, suddenly very attentive.

Merriwig: “Now. Tell me what you’re carrying—truly—and what the Swamp has done to you. And then I’ll tell you what I can do… and what I cannot.”
He gestures to the benches.
Merriwig: “Sit. Drink. And please—don’t lie in my teahouse. It makes the mushrooms sulk.”
